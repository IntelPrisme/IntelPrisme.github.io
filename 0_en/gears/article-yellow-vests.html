The Genesis of the Yellow Vests: Autopsy of an Internal Devaluation

⏱ 4 min read

While the Yellow Vests movement crystallizes decades of territorial fractures, its systemic trigger lies in a brutal sovereign trade-off: the forced transfer of wealth from households to the productive apparatus to mitigate the decline of French competitiveness. In 2019, the decision to finance the continuation of the Competitiveness and Employment Tax Credit (CICE) through a tax on thermal mobility marked the breaking point of social peace.

The Hartz Model: The Precedent of Asymmetry

At the turn of the 2000s, Germany, then dubbed the "sick man of Europe," undertook a strategic mutation to restore its industrial base. Under Chancellor Gerhard Schröder, the Hartz laws constituted a maneuver of internal devaluation: since currency adjustment was impossible within the monetary union, Berlin restored its price competitiveness by radically compressing labor costs and social benefits.

This mercantilist strategy allowed Germany to accumulate record trade surpluses, peaking at nearly 250 billion euros in 2016. However, this gain in external power was bought at the price of a deep social fracture, deliberately sacrificing internal cohesion to guarantee survival in the global economic hierarchy.

The "Fabless" Mirage and Industrial Atrophy

While Germany consolidated its productive core, France strayed into the doctrine of "fabless" companies. This vision postulated that power would henceforth reside only in design and the intangible, delegating production to emerging powers.

This was a geopolitical disaster: by dismantling its assembly lines, France lost thousands of jobs and broke the organic link between its territory and its wealth. The productive apparatus, thus amputated, saw its market shares collapse, leaving the country vulnerable to a German bloc whose power rests precisely on the physical control of industrial transformation.

The 2018 Pivot: The Kohler Arbitration

In 2017, the Macron administration decided to transform the CICE into permanent and direct charge reductions to stop the lag behind Berlin. However, this mutation generated an unprecedented budgetary shock: the "double year."

In 2019, the State had to simultaneously honor old CICE debts and finance new direct exemptions. To fill this 20-billion-euro gap, the executive activated a captive fiscal lever: the carbon tax. By branding this budgetary drain as ecological, the State deliberately transferred the cost of maintaining international competitiveness onto the mobility of the lower and middle classes. This sacrifice of the geographical periphery triggered the Yellow Vests insurrection.

The Hinterland Revolt

The November 2018 uprising marked the failure of technocratic territorial management. Mobility, which the State perceived as a simple fiscal variable, proved to be the vital nerve of "peripheral France." The insurrection was the rebellion of the hinterland against a globalized urban center that no longer provided economic security in exchange for fiscal obedience.

Strategic Failure

To extinguish the fire, the executive had to concede nearly 10 billion euros in emergency measures. This retreat constitutes a disaster: the State spent in one night half of the competitiveness gains it hoped to extract. France found itself doubly weakened: its deficit widened without restoring internal peace, and its "competitiveness shock" was stripped of its financial substance.

The Yellow Vests episode seals France's entry into an era of reformist paralysis. The State now knows it no longer has the "strategic depth" required to impose new sacrifices on its population. France is condemned to a diplomacy of debt to mask its industrial atrophy.
