Orientalism: Western Conceptions of the Orient - Edward W. Said

⏱ 2 min read

Edward Said's work, Orientalism, is a masterful application of the notion of episteme to the field of geopolitics and cultural history. Said demonstrates that "the Orient" is not an objective geographical reality, but an intellectual construction shaped by the West.

The construction of an imaginary "Other"

Said defines orientalism as a style of thought based on an ontological and epistemological distinction made between "the Orient" (them) and "the Occident" (us). The West has created an image of the Orient as irrational, depraved, frozen in time, and mysterious, in opposition to a rational, moral, dynamic, and superior West. This episteme does not seek to understand the Orient as it is, but to define it in relation to the needs of the West.

Knowledge as an instrument of domination

Drawing on the work of Michel Foucault, Said argues that knowledge and power are inseparable. The study of the Orient (languages, customs, history) by European scholars, philologists, and poets was not a disinterested quest for truth. It was a way to "grasp" the Orient intellectually in order to better dominate it politically. By classifying and defining Oriental populations, Europe gave itself the moral and technical right to administer them.

Orientalism as an institution

Orientalism is not merely an opinion; it is an institutionalized system:

  • Academic: Through university chairs and scholarly publications.
  • Imaginary: Through literature (Flaubert, Nerval) and painting.
  • Political: Through colonial administrations.

This network of texts and institutions forms a power structure that filters reality: a diplomat or soldier arriving in the Orient did not see the reality on the ground, but what Orientalist books had taught them to see.

The narrative infrastructure of imperialism

Said's fundamental contribution is to show that imperial domination does not rely solely on cannons and ships, but on the ability to impose one's own narrative. He who has the power to define the other possesses a formidable geopolitical weapon. Orientalism thus provided the intellectual justification necessary for the British and French empires, and later for American foreign policy in the Middle East.

This reading is particularly relevant to analyzing contemporary conflicts, as it invites us to wonder to what extent current geopolitical analyses are still trapped in mental constructs inherited from this colonial episteme.
