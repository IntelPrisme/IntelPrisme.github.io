POLITICS/GEOPOLITICS

The Prince - Niccolò Machiavelli

⏱ 2 min read

Breaking completely with the idealist tradition of his time, The Prince by Niccolò Machiavelli marks the birth of modern political science. The work does not discuss what the State should be, but what it actually is: an arena of struggle for the conquest and preservation of power.

The autonomy of politics

Machiavelli is the first to radically separate politics from morality and religion. For him, politics has its own internal logic. A sovereign who strives to be "good" in a world of predators is doomed to fail. Efficiency becomes the supreme value: an action is not judged "just" or "unjust", but "useful" or "harmful" to the stability of the State. This is the birthplace of the "raison d'état" (reason of state).

The dialectic of Virtù and Fortuna

The Prince's success relies on the balance between two forces:

  • Fortuna: The unpredictable flow of events, comparable to a devastating river. It represents the role of luck and external circumstances.
  • Virtù: This is not moral virtue, but the Prince's energy, strategic intelligence, and capacity for anticipation. The Prince must use his virtù to build dams against fortuna. Machiavelli explains that it is better to be impetuous than cautious, as fortune favors the bold.

The use of force and cunning

The most famous imagery of the work is that of the Prince who must be both lion and fox:

  • The Lion: To frighten the wolves and use brute force when necessary.
  • The Fox: To recognize traps and master the art of dissimulation. Machiavelli postulates that it is better to be feared than loved, if one cannot be both. Love is a bond of gratitude that men break as soon as their own interests are at stake, whereas fear is maintained by a dread of punishment that never leaves the mind.

The economy of violence

Contrary to popular belief, Machiavelli does not advocate gratuitous cruelty. He advises a "cruelty well used": it must be exercised all at once at the beginning of a reign to restore order, so as not to have to repeat it. An excess of initial clemency that leads to chaos is, according to him, more cruel than a targeted execution that ensures civil peace.

The Prince and the People

Despite his reputation for cynicism, Machiavelli emphasizes that the Prince cannot sustainably rule against his people. While he must be wary of the Greats (the elites), whose ambition is to dominate, he must seek the support of the people, whose desire is simply not to be oppressed. A Prince who has the people against him has no bulwark in times of adversity.

In sum, The Prince is a survival manual in a hostile environment. It teaches that the stability of a country justifies the use of means that are sometimes morally condemnable, because the worst immorality in politics remains, for Machiavelli, powerlessness and failure.
