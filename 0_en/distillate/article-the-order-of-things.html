PHILOSOPHY

The Order of Things - Michel Foucault

⏱ 2 min read

In The Order of Things (1966), Michel Foucault proposes an "archaeology" of the human sciences. His goal is to uncover the episteme, that is, the bedrock of unconscious rules which, in a given era, defines what is sayable, thinkable, and true.

1. The Renaissance (up to the mid-17th century): Resemblance

At this time, the world was read like a book. Knowledge was based on similitude. To understand a plant or a star, one looked for signs of resemblance (the signature of God).

  • Logic: Language is part of the world; it is a thing among things.
  • Knowledge: Interpretation and commentary are the primary tools. There is no rupture between words and objects.

2. The Classical Age (mid-17th to 19th century): Representation

The episteme shifts: one no longer seeks resemblance, but order and measurement. Language detaches itself from things to become a tool for classification.

  • The tool: The "Table". We classify nature (Natural History), we analyze currency (Analysis of Wealth), and we structure language (General Grammar).
  • Logic: Knowledge consists of naming correctly and arranging elements in a rational grid to render the world transparent to thought.

3. Modernity (19th century - mid-20th century): History and the End of Man

A second shift occurs. Things are no longer classified on a static table, but acquire historical depth and internal laws.

  • The evolution of knowledge:
    • Grammar becomes Linguistics (language has a life of its own).
    • Analysis of Wealth becomes Economics (production and labor).
    • Natural History becomes Biology (organism and function).
  • The emergence of "Man": It is here that Foucault places his most famous thesis. Before the 19th century, "man" did not exist as an object of study. He appears as a "double figure": he is both the one who knows the world (subject) and a biological or economic object among others (object).

4. The Invention and "Death of Man"

This is the climax and the most complex point of the book. Foucault demonstrates that the human being, as we study him today, is merely a recent creation of this modern episteme.

The appearance of Man (19th century)

Before 1800, "man" did not exist as an object of science. Human nature or the soul was studied, but not Man as a central figure. He suddenly appears as an "empirico-transcendental doublet":

  • He is the subject who looks (the one who creates science).
  • He is the object being looked at (the one that biology, economics, and psychology dissect).

Why speak of his "death"?

When Foucault announces the "death of man", he is predicting the end of an intellectual configuration.

  • Man is a pawn in the system: Foucault wrote during the era of structuralism. He believes that it is not individuals who create history or meaning, but systems that surpass us (language, power relations, the unconscious, the economy).
  • The shift of the episteme: Foucault suggests that if the rules of knowledge change again—for example, if the very structure of language or technology takes precedence over the individual—then the figure of "man" will fade away.

Man is an invention of recent date, as the archaeology of our thought easily shows. And perhaps nearing its end. He is like a face drawn in the sand at the edge of the sea: all it takes is for the episteme to shift for the tide to wash him away, leaving room for new forms of knowledge where the individual is no longer the center.
